The Metamorphosis: a Study in Alienation

19 December 2020

The Metamorphosis is an absurdist novella that deals with alienation. Different critical lenses define 'alienation' differently, yet the story easily lends itself to numerous readings. Kafka purposefully interpolates Marxist, psychoanalytical, and political themes to ridicule destructive cultural and socio-economic norms we consider unconnected byproducts of human nature. This generalization effectively strips away constantly-evolving human constructs to reveal the primal forces of the Anthropocene: our instinct for self-preservation and the often contradicting desire to act on empathy.

Gregor Samsa wakes one morning as a human-sized insect. His body aches as if a reminder of his burdens; Gregor's family enjoys an upper-class lifestyle thanks to Gregor's demanding job as a traveling businessman, where he is pushed to work ever harder by an indifferent employer. Kafka presents us with an archetypal middle class, allured by false promises and kept in line through absurd constructs while well-to-do beneficiaries enjoy the product of its labor. Gregor is constantly traveling, so his family relationship is purely transactional, an important preface to the alienation that takes place so unusually quickly. Still an insect, Gregor grows anxious that his present condition will make it hard to get dressed and catch the early train to work. As is often the case in Kafka's absurdism, a character's unusual reaction to events is a reflection of absurdities contemporary society espouses; Gregor's understated reaction is a reminder that humanity is not an ever-expanding checklist of superfluous attributes one needs to check off to remain in the ever-narrowing "we." Social liberalism advocates for a broad definition of humanity, yet it is precisely the most ardent liberals, the youth, that Kafka blames for allowing injustice to occur.

Each family member grieves the metamorphosis: the father is angry at fate for inconveniencing his comfort, the mother is in denial and pretends the change is only temporary, the younger sister, Grete, doesn't yet realize how this impacts her and tries to help Gregor. She sets out several foods to cater to his new tastes and makes a point of cleaning his room. She does this because she is not immediately affected by the sudden change, allowing egoism to lay dormant and empathy to persist. Sensing that she can't help but be repulsed by his appearance, Gregor hides under a couch where the lack of space suffocates him every time his sister walks in the room. Once he is aware of just what the world sees in him, Gregor tries to mask what he cannot change, to blend in even if through self-harm. Grete tries to do even more for the "monstrous vermin" by removing the furniture. Gregor, desperate to retain his humanity, clings to his favorite portrait, which startles his mother and escalates into a confrontation with the now begrudgingly employed patriarch. During the mayhem, an apple lodges itself in Gregor's carapace. No one bothers to remove the apple, which rots and causes an infection on Gregor's skin. Though still repulsed, the father is overtaken by guilt and allows Gregor's door to remain open in the evenings. Everyone sees a "monstrous vermin" where there is a son and brother, but to calm their moral objections, both parents and Grete take nominal steps while simultaneously looking for any excuse justify their underlying prejudice. An uneasy peace ensues until self-preservation instincts are completely unhinged into malice in the face of financial misfortune that catalyzes the inevitable.

Without Gregor to provide for the entire family, each person has to work. The father, who retired after a failed business and spends his days lounging and reading the paper, has to go back to work. The sister, whose life revolves around playing the violin and other leisurely activities, must suddenly find employment and help her mother, who can no longer be the loving housewife and must also find work. They realize that they can't afford to maintain their social standing but can't bring themselves to move to a smaller flat, instead citing Gregor's supposed inability to travel to a new address; Gregor can easily be packed into a suitcase. The family instinctively projects its frustrations and unrelated woes onto the "monstrous vermin." Gregor's room is rarely cleaned and ends up as storage for forgotten items. Each individual is personally inconvenienced by Gregor's inability to work, so they collectively project their slight discomfort as a sadistic injustice onto their son. Though an individual complaint does not drive any one individual to an act of hate, it is enough to green-light even the most heinous of crimes. In the end, it is Grete who demands Gregor be removed from the home. Separated from her violin, the youngest member of the family is quick to jump from one intense opinion to another. A political reading might see this as a reflection of the youth as a fickle demographic to be manipulated, but the identity of the young — our naivete &mdash speaks to our place within the human species. The young epitomize our ready reliance on emotional judgement that so often escalates tension and leads to radical change. Meanwhile, Gregor had been starving himself to cope with neglect and dies the next day. His family sighs a collective sigh of relief without a hint of remorse. They rest easy, proud in their efforts to tolerate the monstrous vermin in place of Gregor for as long as they did. Perhaps therein lies the problem; To tolerate is to mask an aversion, and masks tend to come off when misfortune befalls the community.

The Metamorphosis is far removed from the strictly grounded worlds of other realists yet retains the key elements of alienation — different factions of a community react in their own ways to ill-informed perceptions about an "other." Gregor is purposefully introduced as "an insect" without a clear description of his physical appearance, an ambiguity no one bothers to resolve through communication. It is a short story written in 1915 that speaks volumes to the future: Jews in Germany, serfs in Russia, African Americans, and now Uighurs in China. Before every tragedy, there is a youth that tries to change things until it is jaded. It is burned out by trying to feel for "monstrous vermin" rather than a brother. Our instinct for self-preservation, when unchecked, results in a language of lodged apples and festering wounds. We're human in many ways, including in our ready submission to the reptilian brain that sees 'different.' It's hard to face the world in all of its complexity; it's hard to keep using that human brain, but the consequences of complacency are, for lack of a better word, Kafkaesque.
