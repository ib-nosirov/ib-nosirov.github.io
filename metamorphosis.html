The Metamorphosis: A Study of Alienation

14 December 2020

The Metamorphosis can be analyzed through many lenses --- one man's poignant's renderings of the first half of the 20st century, an autopsy of 20th century capitalism, and whether he intendend to or not, a timely biopsy of toxic masculinity. I hope to generalize, to the best of my ability, these themes and perhaps point out some reasons for why we humans have such a hard time accepting each other when objectively, it's so clearly in our collective and, for most of us, individual best interest. Franz Kafka's novella is a story of alienation. The main character, Gregor Samsa, wakes one morning as a human-sized insect. He experiences various pains and aches, which lead him to ponder on his life as a travelling business associate at a firm his father is endebted to. He dreams of freedom and grows anxious that his present condition, that of a bug, will make it hard to get dressed and catch the early train to work. Gregor's reaction to his metamophosis seems like a satirical reaction to an absurd situation, but upon closer inspection, it's precisely the rationality that is so offputting. Humanity, Kafka argues, is not an ever expanding checklist of superflous physical attributes one needs to check off to remain in the ever-narrowing "we;" humanity is, in part, the abstract bustle and shared anxieties that make up our collective signature on this pale blue dot. Gregor doesn't realize that there could be anything life-changing about his appearance, nor does he display some extraordinary virtue that might evoke emotion at his later mistreatment. Those we find different don't need some redeeming quality that spares them from our inhuman treatment. Liberalism has propogated these ideas for as long as it's existed, but liberalism is a platform of the young, a fickle group of romantics not yet jaded by fate. As Kafka goes on to argue, attrocities happen when once proud allies suddenly change their tune. Gregor is permanently an insect and as the family deludes itself with a hope of its war-veteran bread-winner's return, we are acquainted with humanity in all of its colors. As the family tries to adjust to Gregor's metamorphosis, miscommunication and good-intentioned compromise slowly devolve into a campaign to dehumanize Gregor. Grete Samsa, Gregor's sister is the first family member to care for her brother after he is abused by the father. She sets out several foods to cater to his new tastes and makes a point of cleaning his room. Sensing that she can't help but be repulsed by his appearance, Gregor hides under a couch and drapes covers over himself so as to be completely invsible when Grete enters his room. Grete tries to do even more for her brother and tries to remove the furniture so he can more easily climb the walls and ceiling. Gregor, still hoping of returning to human form, tries to protect his humanity and leans on his favorite portrait to commuicate his desire, which startles his mother and leads to a confrontation with the patriarch of the family. Even as Gregor tries to prove his best intentions, his father bombards him with apples, one of which lodges itself in the insect carapace. No one bothers to remove the apple, which rots and causes an infection on Gregor's skin. The father is overtaken by guilt and allows Gregor's door to remain open in the evenings, thereby acknowledging Gregor as a member of the family. Though no one sees Gregor as human, they all try, despite their aversions, despite their fear of the unknown, despite their anxieties about the future, to take care of Gregor, who in turn replies with accomodation and understanding. But these are humans and they inevitably break; they break and they keep going. That is until economics catalyzes the inevitable. Without Gregor to provide for the entire family, each person has to work and even then, their quality of life diminishes. The father, who retired after a failed business and spends his days lounging and reading the paper, has to go back to work. The sister whose life revolves around playing violin and other leisurely activities must suddenly find employement and help her mother, who can no longer be the loving housewife. They realize that they can't afford to maintain their lifestyle, but can't bring themselves to to move to a smaller flat, instead citing Gregor's supposed inability to travel to a new address; Gregor can easily be packed into a suitcase. The family then actively tries to dehumanize and neglect Gregor as the reason for all their troubles. His room is rarely cleaned and ends up as a storage for forgotten items. Each individual is personally inconvienced by Gregor's inabilty to work, so they collectively project their slight discomfort as a sadistic injustice onto their son. In the end, it is Grete who demands Gregor be removed from the home. Meanwhile, Gregor had been starving himself to cope with neglect, and dies the next day. His family sighes a collective sigh of relief without a hint of remorse. Gregor was the travelling son whose Herculean efforts, at the detriment of his health, built everything the family knew and when he was no longer able to provide value, he was gradually discarded. Every individual in the story, however, could have claimed to tolerate Gregor. Perhaps therein lies the problem. To tolerate is to mask an aversion and masks have a tendency of coming off when misfortune befalls the community. Kafka might have seen in Gregor the Jews of Germany, who were skilled workers that participated in WWI when called upon, the serfs of Russia who held up the empire and its imperialist endeavors, the African Americans who made cotton king. Today, it's Uighurs enslaved in sweatshops on the basis of faith, African Americans whose backs fester with 400 years of apples lodged and left to rot, rendering them "incompatible" with the suburban dream. The tragedy is that for every attrocity, there was a youth that tried, there was a patriarchy that pretend to make concessions, but it's ultimately the folly of seeing the "monstrous vermin" where there is a son that allowed these negative sentiments to prevail. We're human in many ways, including in our ready submission to the reptilian brain that sees difference. It's hard to face the world in all of its complexity; it's hard to keeping using that human brain, but it's Kafkaesque not to.
