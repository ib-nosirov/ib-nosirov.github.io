Friday reflection: quantifying habits, concise proofs are hard, and psets

The writing debrief I tried to do last week fell through. I think it's because the weekend, instead of the weekdays, should be the time for all homework assignnments; it's hard to reflect on the day when you have a deadline in two hours and tomorrow's work is cut out for you. I find myself in a reactionary mode and that's exhausting, which leads to more time wasted trying to relieve the fatigue with too much low-quality entertainment at the wrong time of day. No more. Starting this week, I'm going to complete all my homework on the weekend so there is a) more time to react to activities during the week and b) it's much easier to write without anxiety in the face of impending doom that is a deadline. With tons of work and the subsequent escapist cycle of binging nothing in particular, I've realized, tracking habits is much more stressful than it really should be. This habit tracker idea is great because it helps quantify just how little our accomplishments are in the grand scheme of a month or year. I happen to thrive on negative reinforcement, so I rather enjoy seeing a feeble streak of 5 runs because it's a sort of launching pad for a more "perfect week". Addressing habit tracking more generally, I think it's only useful as raw data that should inform how tasks are managed, but it might be unhealthy to attach emotional value to habit tracking simply because numbers have the effect of describing the past and spitting out bleak self-fulfilling prophecies. In short, will power is not a matter of looking at how much sugar you already consumed; rather, will is an exercise of emotional obsession, like desperately holding on to something. Next week, I intend to keep track of exercise, 'perfect meals', and the number of pomodoros. Simply as a gauge, not a motivational tool.

Proofs

Prove that there is not rational number whose square root is $12$.


Suppose $m^2 = 12n^2$, where $m$ and $n$ have no common factor. It follows that $m$ must be even, and therefore $n$ must be odd. Let $m = 2r$. Then we have $r^2 = 3n^2$, so that $r$ is also odd. Let $r = 2s + 1$ and $n = 2t + 1$. Then $$4s^2 + 4s + 1 = 3(4t^2 + 4t + 1) = 12t^2 + 12t + 3,$$ so that $$4(s^2 + s - 3t^2 - 3t) = 2.$$ But this is absurd since $2$ cannot be a multiple of $4$. QED

This proof wastes no words while also having a flow to it. Most importantly, it makes sense, something that I've been struggling with in my own proofs. That shift from the first sentence to the second is the perfect logical leap — we know that $m$ must be even because otherwise it must be odd and that can't be the case since 12 contains a 2 and the equality would break down as an odd number can't contain a 2. The beauty of this leap is that it forces the reader to think, but not assume anything other than the definitions of odd and even. I hope to get this good at proofs by the start of the fall semester, when I'm hoping to take real analysis. To do this, I need to work problems from Rudin and, more importantly, analyze them every day.
