Linear Inverse Problems

September 01, 2021

Consider a linear transformation $A$ applied on a space of vectors $x.$ The linear transformation maps the space of $x$ to some other space $y.$ To determine $y$ given $x$ is a forward problem. To determine $x$ given $y$ is an inverse problem. As is commonly introduced in linear algebra, the inverse problem can be formulated as a linear system of the form $Ax=y,$ where $A$ is of size $m\times n$. When $m>n,$ the system is overdetermined and has infinitely many solutions. When $n>m,$ the system is underdetermined and has no solutions. Even if a system has no solutions, it is guaranteed to still have a pseudoinverse soluton â€” a solution which minimizes the $\ell_2$ norm. Finally, depending on the stability of a solution, we classify inverse problems as well-posed or ill-posed.
