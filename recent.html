Meditations: Books 1-3 Analysis

12 June 2020

In a world where you can be anything, be kind. A cliché phrase that comes oh so close to encapsulating the essence of stoicism. The reason that this word or any other will not fully encapsulate the essence of the stoicism is because of the ambiguity in the word `virtue.' Throughout the Meditations, Aurelius uses virtue interchangeably with 'in accordance with nature' --- a phrase equally ambiguous. Whether the phrase is subjective or not raises the question of whether it is rational to assume that humans naturally know the difference between right and wrong. Assuming that there is a right or wrong and that humans are naturally encoded with it would mean that the notion of arete, or virtue, need not be explicitly defined. Whether they needed to or not, the Greeks defined it as knowledge of knowledge. The ultimate knowledge comes from trying to understand the nature of knowledge and that the exercises of the mind, body, and soul are but vehicles on the road to that understanding. On the other hand if humans are not born with the inherent ability to discern between virtue vice, they are constantly exposed to nature and it is man's choice to learn from nature and live by the laws of nature. This is assuming that nature does in fact have some laws, though this assumption is quickly proven correct when one but observes that there are distinct patterns within the interactions in the universe itself. Where these patterns originate from cannot be proven because there is no way for the rational mind to escape the container of our universe and look beyond to see whether a creator does indeed exist. Thus we are eternally bound to our perceptions of nature and notions of right and wrong that ultimately originate from the word of man, regardless of whether that word is really that of a divine creator.
So we return to the notion of kindness with respect to man's relationship with nature. Not kindness alone, but also that of reason for reason is the guide for the force of virtue that is kindness. For instance, kindness might be the sparing of a single life because nature deems life to be sacred, but if the sparing of that one life leads to the loss of two lives, it is more kind to take the single life. Kindness extends to kindness toward oneself as that too is a law of nature, namely that all living things that have been granted life should try to preserve it. An exercise in reason also might reveal nuances and irrationalities that might arise on the way to making the virtuous and kind decision in any situation. Therefore, reason must be frequently exercised and knowledge frequently expanded. One must continually invest in oneself to live a life of virtue. This means the exercise of the mind and body so that the flesh is primed for the exercise of the soul, which is done in no other way but in kind acts, such as temperance or acts of charity.
Different acts can serve as both exercises of the body, mind, and soul. Temperance is one such exercise that calluses the mind, and is often in line with what is naturally beneficially to the body. It's effect on the soul is also present, as it is a kindness extended toward oneself, but it must genuine as an act of temperance with a vain intention is in fact just an act of vanity.
The question arises: what action, if any, brings the most exercise to the soul? Since we abide in the natural world, only actions that are consistent with nature's laws, which are most evident to us human beings are the most beneficial. Any act of kindness toward oneself cannot benefit anyone but one individual, so an act of kindness toward many will benefit the soul and bring the subject closer to being one with nature than any act.
